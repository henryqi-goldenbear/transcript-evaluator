INTERVIEWER:
That’s great context—thanks for that. You mentioned the seller onboarding project at ShopHop. Can you walk me through that in a bit more detail? What was the problem, what did you do, and what was the outcome?

CANDIDATE:
Absolutely. When I joined ShopHop, our seller onboarding flow was a major pain point. We had a 40% drop-off rate during the process, and the sellers who did complete it were taking an average of 12 days to go live. This was hurting our supply side, which directly impacted demand—fewer sellers meant fewer listings, which meant fewer buyers. The problem was twofold: the flow was too long and cumbersome, and it wasn’t clear to sellers what the value was at each step.

I started by digging into the data to identify where the biggest drop-offs were happening. We found that the tax ID verification step was a huge hurdle—sellers were abandoning because they didn’t have their documents ready or didn’t understand why we needed them. I also ran a survey with sellers who had dropped off to understand their pain points better. The feedback was clear: they wanted a simpler, more transparent process with clear milestones.

I proposed a redesign that broke the flow into smaller, more digestible steps, with clear progress indicators and tooltips explaining why each piece of information was necessary. We also introduced a “save and continue later” feature, which was a big ask from sellers. To validate the changes, we ran an A/B test with 10% of new sellers. The test showed a 22% increase in conversion and a 15% reduction in time to go live. We also saw a 30% drop in support tickets related to onboarding, which was a huge win for our customer support team.

The rollout was a bit bumpy—we had to coordinate with engineering, legal, and customer support to make sure everyone was aligned—but ultimately, it was a success. The key was keeping the seller’s perspective front and center and using data to drive decisions.

INTERVIEWER:
That’s a really strong example. I’m curious—how did you handle the pushback from legal on the tax ID verification step? I imagine they were hesitant to make any changes there.

CANDIDATE:
Great question. Legal was definitely concerned about compliance, and rightfully so. Their initial stance was that we couldn’t remove or simplify the tax ID step because it was a regulatory requirement. I approached this by first acknowledging their concerns and then framing the conversation around the *how* rather than the *what*. Instead of asking if we could remove the step, I asked if we could explore ways to make it less painful for sellers while still meeting compliance needs.

I worked with them to understand the exact requirements and then brainstormed solutions. One idea was to move the tax ID step later in the flow, after sellers had already seen some value—like setting up their storefront or listing their first product. This way, they’d be more invested in completing the process. Legal was open to testing this as long as we maintained the same level of verification. We also added a clear explanation of why the tax ID was necessary and how it would be used, which helped reduce confusion.

In the end, the A/B test showed that moving the step later in the flow didn’t increase drop-off at that stage, and it actually improved overall conversion. Legal was happy because compliance wasn’t compromised, and sellers were happier because the process felt smoother. It was a win-win.

INTERVIEWER:
That’s a great example of navigating stakeholder concerns. Shifting gears a bit—Hearthside’s marketplace is all about connecting homeowners with local service providers. Can you talk about a time when you had to make a tough trade-off between supply and demand? How did you approach it?

CANDIDATE:
Sure. At Storied, we had a situation where our demand side—brands looking for creators—was growing much faster than our supply side. We had a backlog of brand requests, but we didn’t have enough creators in certain niches to fulfill them. This was creating a lot of frustration on both sides: brands were threatening to churn because they couldn’t find the right creators, and creators were feeling overwhelmed by the volume of requests.

I had to make a call on where to focus our resources. One option was to double down on creator acquisition—running targeted campaigns, improving onboarding, and offering incentives to creators in high-demand niches. The other option was to temporarily limit brand access to certain niches until we could build up supply. This was a tough trade-off because limiting brand access could hurt revenue and growth in the short term, but it would also prevent brand churn and ensure a better experience for creators.

I decided to go with a hybrid approach. We temporarily limited brand access to the most oversubscribed niches while simultaneously ramping up creator acquisition efforts. We also introduced a “waitlist” feature for brands, which gave them visibility into when they could expect to connect with creators in their niche. This helped manage expectations and reduced churn.

To measure the impact, we tracked a few key metrics: brand churn rate, creator satisfaction scores, and the time it took for brands to get matched with creators. After three months, we saw a 15% reduction in brand churn, and creator satisfaction scores improved by 10 points. The waitlist also gave us valuable data on which niches to prioritize for creator acquisition.

It wasn’t a perfect solution—some brands were still frustrated—but it was the right trade-off for the long-term health of the marketplace. The key was being transparent with both sides and using data to guide the decision.

INTERVIEWER:
That’s a really thoughtful approach. I’m curious—how did you communicate this decision to the brands? I imagine that was a delicate conversation.

CANDIDATE:
It was definitely delicate. We started by segmenting the brands based on their impact and potential churn risk. For our high-value brands, we set up 1:1 calls with their account managers to explain the situation and the steps we were taking to address it. For the broader group, we sent a transparent email outlining the changes, the reasons behind them, and the timeline for when they could expect access to creators in their niche.

The email was key because it set the tone. We framed it as a temporary measure to improve the overall experience for both brands and creators. We also highlighted the waitlist feature as a way to give them visibility and control. The feedback was mixed—some brands were understanding, while others were frustrated—but the transparency helped a lot. We also made sure to follow up with regular updates on our progress, which kept them engaged.

INTERVIEWER:
That makes sense. Let’s talk about experimentation. Can you walk me through a time when an experiment you ran didn’t go as planned? What did you learn from it?

CANDIDATE:
Sure. At Storied, we ran an experiment to test a new pricing model for our creator marketplace. The idea was to introduce a tiered pricing structure based on creator engagement metrics, with the goal of increasing revenue while incentivizing creators to be more active on the platform. We hypothesized that creators who were more engaged would be willing to pay a higher fee for premium features, like advanced analytics and priority matching with brands.

We rolled out the experiment to 20% of our creator base and tracked a few key metrics: revenue per creator, creator retention, and brand satisfaction. The results were… not what we expected. Revenue per creator did increase, but creator retention dropped by 12%, and brand satisfaction scores fell because fewer creators were available for matching.

The issue was that we hadn’t fully considered the impact on the supply side. The tiered pricing model created a perception of inequality among creators—those in the lower tiers felt like they were being penalized for not being “engaged enough,” even if they were still providing value to the platform. We also underestimated how much brands relied on a diverse pool of creators, not just the top-tier ones.

We ended up rolling back the experiment and went back to the drawing board. The key lesson was to always pressure-test assumptions with both sides of the marketplace. We also realized that we needed to do more qualitative research—surveys and interviews—to understand the emotional and psychological impact of changes, not just the quantitative metrics.

From there, we pivoted to a different approach: introducing optional premium features that creators could opt into, rather than a mandatory tiered structure. This gave creators more control and reduced the perception of inequality. The next experiment we ran with this approach showed much better results—revenue increased without hurting retention or brand satisfaction.

After a few months, she was much better at managing her workload. She even started mentoring other junior team members on prioritization, which was a great sign of her growth. This was a major issue, so we had to act fast. I called an emergency roadmap review with my team and key stakeholders. At the same time, we launched a deeper investigation into the root cause of the issue. We realized that the algorithm change had been made without fully considering the impact on our top creators. To prevent this from happening again, we introduced a new process where any changes to the algorithm or content discovery system would require a cross-functional review, including input from the creator success team.

The key takeaway for me was the importance of being agile and willing to reprioritize when the data tells you something isn’t working. It’s not always easy to deprioritize work that the team has already invested in, but it’s necessary to keep the product healthy.
