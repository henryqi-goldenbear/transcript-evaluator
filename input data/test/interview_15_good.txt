INTERVIEWER:
That’s a great example of removing friction. Let’s wrap up with a technical question. How do you think about measuring the health of a two-sided marketplace?

CANDIDATE:
Measuring the health of a two-sided marketplace is all about balancing supply and demand while ensuring both sides are getting value. I usually start with a few core metrics:

1. **Liquidity**: This is the most critical metric for any marketplace. I look at things like match rate (the percentage of demand that gets fulfilled) and time-to-match (how quickly supply can meet demand). For example, at Stripe, we tracked how quickly sellers could start accepting payments after signing up, because that was a leading indicator of whether they’d stick around.

2. **Retention**: For both sides of the marketplace, I look at retention curves to understand how sticky the platform is. For Hearthside, that might mean tracking how often homeowners return to book services or how many service providers stay active on the platform over time.

3. **Unit economics**: This is especially important for scaling. I look at metrics like customer acquisition cost (CAC), lifetime value (LTV), and take rate (the percentage of each transaction that the platform keeps). The goal is to ensure that the marketplace is sustainable and that both sides are getting enough value to justify their participation.

4. **Quality signals**: For a marketplace like Hearthside, trust is critical. I’d look at metrics like ratings and reviews, dispute rates, and response times to ensure that both homeowners and service providers are having positive experiences.

5. **Growth loops**: I also think about how the marketplace grows organically. For example, do happy homeowners refer their friends? Do service providers bring their existing clients onto the platform? These loops can be powerful drivers of sustainable growth.

The key is to track these metrics over time and look for leading indicators of problems. For example, if match rates start to decline, it might signal that supply is drying up, which could lead to lower retention on the demand side.
