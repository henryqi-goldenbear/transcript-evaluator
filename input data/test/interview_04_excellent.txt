INTERVIEWER:
Let’s talk about experimentation. How do you design an A/B test for a feature that could have asymmetric effects on the two sides of a marketplace?

CANDIDATE:
Great question. Asymmetric effects are one of the trickiest parts of running experiments in a two-sided marketplace, because a change that benefits one side can often hurt the other. The first step is to define your primary and secondary metrics upfront, and make sure they capture the health of both sides of the marketplace.

For example, when I was at DoorDash, we were testing a feature that let consumers tip Dashers *after* delivery, rather than at checkout. The hypothesis was that this would increase tips, which would improve Dasher earnings and retention. But we knew it could also create a perverse incentive for Dashers to pressure consumers for tips, which would hurt the consumer experience.

We designed the experiment with three primary metrics: (1) average tip amount per order, (2) Dasher retention rate, and (3) consumer NPS. We also tracked secondary metrics like order completion rate, Dasher deactivation rate, and consumer reorder rate. To account for the asymmetry, we segmented the results by both sides of the marketplace. For consumers, we looked at NPS and reorder rate by whether they had tipped in previous orders. For Dashers, we looked at retention and deactivation rates by their average tip amount.

The experiment ran for four weeks, and the results were mixed. Tip amounts increased by 12%, and Dasher retention improved by 5%. But consumer NPS dropped by 3 points, and we saw a 7% increase in customer support tickets related to Dasher behavior. The segmentation showed that the NPS drop was driven entirely by consumers who had tipped less than $2 in previous orders—these consumers were now being asked to tip more aggressively, and they were unhappy about it.

We decided not to launch the feature as-is, but we didn’t scrap it entirely. Instead, we iterated on the design to address the asymmetry. We added a feature that let consumers opt out of post-delivery tipping, and we built a machine learning model to predict which consumers were likely to tip and which weren’t, so we could tailor the experience. We ran another experiment with these changes, and this time, tip amounts increased by 8%, Dasher retention improved by 4%, and consumer NPS was flat. We launched the feature, and over the next year, Dasher earnings increased by 6% with no negative impact on consumer satisfaction.

The key takeaway is that you can’t treat a two-sided marketplace as a single population. You have to segment your analysis and design your experiment to capture the tradeoffs between the two sides. And sometimes, the right answer isn’t to launch or kill the feature—it’s to iterate and find the equilibrium.

Shoppers were wasting time running around the store looking for items that weren’t where the app said they’d be.

I had to pull the feature after two weeks and issue an apology to the affected shoppers. It was a humbling experience, especially because I’d been so confident in the backtest results. The postmortem revealed two big mistakes: (1) we hadn’t validated the model’s assumptions with real shoppers before scaling, and (2) we hadn’t built in a feedback loop to detect when the model’s predictions were wrong.

The biggest lesson was the importance of *humility* in experimentation. It’s easy to get attached to your hypothesis, especially when the backtests look good. But in a marketplace, the real world is always more complex than your model. After this, I instituted a rule with my teams: no experiment scales beyond 1% of traffic without a live validation step where we watch real users interact with the feature and collect qualitative feedback. It’s slower, but it’s saved us from a few more failures since then.
