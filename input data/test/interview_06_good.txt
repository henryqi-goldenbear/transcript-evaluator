The key takeaway for me was the importance of looking beyond the primary metric. We’d been so focused on host earnings that we missed the downstream impact on guest behavior. Now, I always make sure to define guardrail metrics upfront to catch these kinds of trade-offs.

INTERVIEWER:

CANDIDATE:
Balancing the needs of both sides is one of the trickiest parts of working on a marketplace, but it’s also what makes the role so interesting. The key is to find ways to align incentives so that what’s good for one side is also good for the other—or at least doesn’t harm them. At Airbnb, we faced this tension when we were redesigning the payout system for experiences. Hosts wanted faster payouts—they’d often complain that waiting 24 hours after an experience to get paid was too long, especially for hosts who relied on that income for cash flow. But from Airbnb’s perspective, faster payouts meant higher fraud risk and more operational complexity.

I started by talking to hosts to understand their pain points. Many of them were small business owners who needed the cash to pay for supplies or staff for their next experience. At the same time, I worked with our fraud and operations teams to understand the constraints. The biggest concern was that faster payouts would make it harder to detect and reverse fraudulent transactions.

We landed on a solution that gave hosts more control over their payout timing without increasing fraud risk. We introduced an option for hosts to request an instant payout for a small fee, while keeping the standard 24-hour payout free. This way, hosts who needed the cash immediately could get it, but the default remained the safer, slower option. We also built in safeguards, like limiting instant payouts to hosts with a strong track record and capping the amount they could withdraw instantly.

The result was a win-win: host satisfaction with payouts improved by 25%, and we didn’t see any increase in fraud. It was a great example of how you can design incentives to align both sides of the marketplace.

That’s a great self-awareness. Let’s shift to the role here at Hearthside. One of the challenges we’re facing is reducing no-shows for booked services. How would you approach this problem?

CANDIDATE:
No-shows are a classic marketplace problem—they hurt both sides of the platform. Customers are left without the service they paid for, and providers lose out on earnings and time. My approach would start with understanding the root causes. Are no-shows happening because customers forget about their appointments, or because they’re not fully committed to the service? Are providers canceling last-minute because they’re overbooked or because they’re not incentivized to show up?

I’d start by digging into the data. How often are no-shows happening, and are there patterns? For example, do they happen more often with certain types of services, or at certain times of day? I’d also talk to both customers and providers to understand their pain points. For customers, is the booking process too frictionless, leading to impulse bookings they later regret? For providers, are they canceling because they’re double-booked or because they’re not earning enough per job?

Once I had a clearer picture, I’d look for solutions that align incentives. For example, we could introduce a small deposit at booking to increase customer commitment, or we could offer providers a bonus for showing up on time. We could also experiment with reminders—maybe a text message 24 hours before the appointment isn’t enough, and we need to add a follow-up call or a more personalized reminder.

I’d also look at the provider side. Are we doing enough to help them manage their schedules? If providers are canceling because they’re overbooked, maybe we need to build better tools for them to block off time or set capacity limits. The key is to find solutions that make it easier for both sides to follow through on their commitments.
