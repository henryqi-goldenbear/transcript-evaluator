To mitigate that, I’d set up a buffer in the Prometheus remote write configuration—maybe 10-15 minutes of local storage. That way, if the network is flaky or the central store is temporarily unavailable, we don’t lose data. Looking back, I made a few mistakes. First, I underestimated the log volume—we were generating way more logs than I anticipated. Second, I didn’t design for scalability. After the outage, I worked with the team to redesign the pipeline. We switched to Loki, which is more scalable and better suited for Kubernetes logs. We also set up separate Loki instances for different teams, so one team’s log volume wouldn’t impact others. And we implemented log retention policies to keep costs under control.

The experience taught me the importance of designing for scale from the start, even if you don’t think you’ll need it. It also reinforced the value of involving stakeholders early and often.
