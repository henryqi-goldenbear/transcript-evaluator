Sure. At Streamify, our main offline metric was AUC, but it didn’t correlate with watch time or retention. INTERVIEWER:
Nice outcome. Walk me through the technical trade-offs you considered when you built that feature store at VideoFlow.

CANDIDATE:
We had three main options: a key-value store like DynamoDB, a time-series database like Influx, or a purpose-built feature store like Feast. DynamoDB was fast but didn’t support time-travel for training data, so we’d have to replay logs. Influx gave us time-travel but couldn’t handle the QPS we needed for online serving. Feast was still early-stage, so we forked it and added a read-through cache in Redis. The trade-off was higher operational overhead—we had to run two clusters—but we got sub-100 ms P99 latency and consistent training-serving data. We also added a background materialization job that compacted features every 15 minutes to keep Redis memory usage under control.

INTERVIEWER:
How did you measure the success of that migration?

CANDIDATE:
We tracked three metrics: online latency, training-serving skew, and model performance. Latency dropped from 450 ms to 90 ms P99. Skew, measured as the KL divergence between online and offline feature distributions, went from 0.12 to 0.01. And because the model now saw the same features at training and inference, NDCG improved by 0.08. We also measured the reduction in launch failures—before the migration, 20 % of launches had to be rolled back due to feature mismatch; after, it was 2 %.

Launches became one-click, and the time dropped to 5 minutes. More importantly, the number of bad launches dropped from 15 % to 2 %. The team adopted it for all models, and it’s now part of the standard ML toolkit.

CANDIDATE:

I learned to always start with a small canary—1 % of traffic—and to monitor not just the target metric but also auxiliary metrics like session length. CANDIDATE:
