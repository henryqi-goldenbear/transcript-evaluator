# Interview transcript fixture
# Role: Backend Software Engineer, Platform team @ Northline Financial
# Quality target: mediocre candidate performance
# Format: INTERVIEWER / CANDIDATE turns

INTERVIEWER:
Hi Jordan, thanks for joining. How is your day going so far?

CANDIDATE:
Pretty good, thanks. Busy morning, but all good.

INTERVIEWER:
Before we get into the details, can you give me the short version of your background?

CANDIDATE:
Sure. I have about five years of backend experience. I have worked mostly on APIs and internal tools. My most recent role was at FinBridge, where I worked on payments-related services. I have used Python, some Go, Postgres, and a bit of AWS. I would say I am a solid backend engineer who likes solving practical problems and working with teams.

INTERVIEWER:
Walk me through your resume and the main roles that are relevant for this position.

CANDIDATE:
I started at a small software company after college doing general backend work. Then I moved to FinBridge, which is more in the fintech space. At FinBridge I was on a team that handled transaction reporting and some partner integrations. I built endpoints, fixed bugs, and helped with a few migrations. I have not always owned huge projects end to end, but I have contributed to a lot of them.

INTERVIEWER:
What drew you to this role and to Northline?

CANDIDATE:
The role seems aligned with my background. I have worked around payments and data consistency, and this sounds like more of that. I also saw that Northline is growing and has interesting technical problems. I do not know every detail of your architecture yet, but the platform engineering part stood out to me.

INTERVIEWER:
How have you used Go in production?

CANDIDATE:
I have used Go on a couple services, mostly maintaining existing code. I wrote some handlers, added logging, and fixed a few bugs around retries. I am more comfortable in Python, but I can work in Go. I know the basics like interfaces, contexts, and goroutines, though I would not say I am an expert in concurrency.

INTERVIEWER:
Tell me about a time you led a project under a tight deadline.

CANDIDATE:
At FinBridge we had to update an integration for a partner API before they deprecated the old version. I was one of the main engineers on it. The deadline was coming up quickly, maybe a month or so. I helped break down the tasks, implemented some endpoint changes, and coordinated with QA. We shipped it before the deadline. It was stressful, but it worked out.

INTERVIEWER:
What specifically did you own on that project?

CANDIDATE:
I owned the mapping changes for two of the partner response types and wrote some tests for them. I also helped debug a production issue after launch where one optional field was coming through empty. I was not the official lead, but I was responsible for a decent chunk of the work.

INTERVIEWER:
Describe a situation where you had to persuade a skeptical stakeholder.

CANDIDATE:
There was a time when product wanted a dashboard change faster than engineering thought was safe. I explained that we needed more time to do it properly. I do not remember all the details, but I showed them that rushing it might create rework. Eventually they agreed to give us a bit more time.

INTERVIEWER:
What evidence did you use to convince them?

CANDIDATE:
Mostly examples from previous dashboard bugs. We had a few cases where metrics were confusing because the definitions were not clear. I pulled up one ticket where support had to explain a wrong-looking number to customers. It was not a formal analysis, but it helped make the risk more concrete.

INTERVIEWER:
Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a teammate.

CANDIDATE:
I had a disagreement with another engineer about whether to add unit tests for a helper function or just cover it through integration tests. I thought unit tests would be useful, and he thought it was unnecessary. We went back and forth a bit. Eventually we compromised and added a smaller set of unit tests.

INTERVIEWER:
How did you handle the conflict in the moment?

CANDIDATE:
I probably could have handled it better. At first I pushed my point too hard in the pull request comments. After that I messaged him directly and we talked through the concern. It ended fine, but I learned that PR comments can sound sharper than intended.

INTERVIEWER:
Tell me about a time you failed or made a mistake.

CANDIDATE:
I once deployed a change that caused a reporting job to miss some records. It was not a huge outage, but it created manual cleanup work. The issue was that I assumed a timestamp field was always present, and it was not. I rolled back the change and added a null check later.

INTERVIEWER:
What did you change afterward to prevent that kind of issue?

CANDIDATE:
I added a test case for missing timestamps and updated the runbook with the symptom. I also started checking edge cases more carefully when dealing with partner data. I would not say it transformed the whole process, but it made me more cautious.

INTERVIEWER:
Tell me about a time you improved a process.

CANDIDATE:
Our deploy process had a few manual steps that people sometimes forgot. I wrote a script to automate part of it. It saved some time and made deployments easier.

INTERVIEWER:
Can you be more specific about what the script did and how much time it saved?

CANDIDATE:
It checked that migrations had run, confirmed the service health endpoint, and posted the deployment version to Slack. Before that, people copied commands from a wiki. It probably saved ten or fifteen minutes per deploy and reduced small mistakes, though I do not have a precise metric.

INTERVIEWER:
Tell me about a time you mentored someone or helped them grow.

CANDIDATE:
I helped a newer engineer get familiar with our codebase. I reviewed their pull requests and answered questions. They got more comfortable over time.

INTERVIEWER:
What was your actual approach to mentoring them?

CANDIDATE:
I set up a weekly thirty-minute pairing session for a few weeks. We walked through one service together, and I asked them to explain changes before opening PRs. I also gave more context in reviews instead of just saying what to change. It helped, but I did not have a formal mentorship plan.

INTERVIEWER:
What is your greatest weakness?

CANDIDATE:
I can get too focused on finishing my own tasks and not communicate early enough when something is slipping. I have improved on that by sending status updates sooner, but it is still something I watch.

INTERVIEWER:
How do you handle competing priorities when everything is urgent?

CANDIDATE:
I try to compare impact and deadlines, then talk to my manager if there is a conflict. I usually ask which item matters most to the business. I am not always perfect at it, but I try not to quietly work late on everything.

INTERVIEWER:
Can you give me a past example when you had to do that?

CANDIDATE:
Earlier this year I had a partner bug, a reporting cleanup task, and an internal tool request all due around the same time. I asked my manager to confirm priority. The partner bug came first because it affected customer data, then the reporting cleanup, and the tool request moved by a week. I fixed the bug in two days and avoided overcommitting.

INTERVIEWER:
Tell me about a time you showed leadership when you were not the formal lead.

CANDIDATE:
During a release, a few people were confused about who was handling rollback checks. I started a shared checklist and asked people to mark what they had verified. It was not a big incident, but it helped the release go more smoothly.

INTERVIEWER:
How do you typically work with product managers?

CANDIDATE:
I try to understand what they actually need, not just the ticket wording. Sometimes I ask clarifying questions if acceptance criteria are vague. I have had good relationships with PMs, though I am still learning how to push back earlier when scope is unclear.

INTERVIEWER:
What questions do you have for us?

CANDIDATE:
I would like to know how your platform team handles on-call and how much project ownership engineers usually get.

INTERVIEWER:
We can talk more about that in the next round. Thanks for the conversation today.

Thanks. I appreciate the time.
